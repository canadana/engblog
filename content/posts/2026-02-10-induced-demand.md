## The pattern

A corridor gets congested. The response is often to add a lane. Travel time improves for a period of months to a few years, then congestion returns to roughly the pre-widening level, even though nothing else about the corridor changed. This has been observed repeatedly enough across independent projects that it has a name: induced demand.

## Why this isn't a modeling failure

The mistake is treating traffic volume as a fixed quantity that a wider road simply moves faster. Volume on a corridor is a function of the *cost* of using it, and travel time is one of the largest components of that cost. When you cut travel time, you lower the effective cost of trips that were previously marginal: the discretionary errand, the slightly-longer commute that becomes tolerable, the trip that shifts from an alternate route, off-peak departure, or another mode entirely.

Three demand sources typically absorb new capacity:

- **Route diversion** — drivers who were avoiding the corridor via a parallel route return to it.
- **Time-of-day shift** — trips that were scheduled outside peak hours to avoid congestion move back into the peak.
- **Latent and induced trips** — trips that would not have occurred at all at the old travel-time cost, including longer-distance commuting enabled by a faster corridor, now occur.

The elasticity of vehicle-miles-traveled with respect to lane-miles added has been estimated in the range of roughly 0.3 to 1.0 in the transportation economics literature, depending on the study, time horizon, and whether the network is already capacity-constrained elsewhere — meaning a meaningful fraction to nearly all of the added capacity gets absorbed by new or shifted vehicle travel rather than converting into a lasting travel-time improvement for existing trips.

## What this implies for analysis, not just conclusions

This doesn't mean added capacity is never justified — it means the correct evaluation metric is not "does travel time improve initially" but "does the corridor's people-moving or goods-moving capacity meet forecast demand once induced and shifted trips are accounted for." A capacity project evaluated only on short-run travel time will look far more successful in its first year than in its fifth.

The same elasticity logic runs in reverse for capacity *removal* — road diets and lane reallocation to transit or active modes tend to show smaller congestion increases than a fixed-demand model predicts, for the same underlying reason: some of the displaced trips don't relocate onto parallel routes, they shift mode, shift time, or stop occurring.
