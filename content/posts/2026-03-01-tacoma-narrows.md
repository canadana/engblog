## What failed

The original Tacoma Narrows Bridge opened on July 1, 1940, and collapsed on November 7, 1940 — a service life of a little over four months. The structure was a suspension bridge with a main span of 2,800 feet, but its deck was only 8 feet deep for that span length, roughly a third of the depth-to-span ratio used on comparable bridges of the era. That shallow, narrow deck gave the roadway very little torsional stiffness.

## Why it failed

Suspension bridge decks are flexible by design; the problem at Tacoma Narrows wasn't flexibility itself but the specific way the deck responded to wind. Even moderate, steady crosswinds — around 40 mph on the day of collapse — triggered a self-sustaining twisting motion known as aeroelastic flutter. As the deck twisted, the airflow separating off its edges fed energy back into the twisting motion rather than damping it out. Each cycle added more energy than the structure's damping removed, so the oscillation amplitude grew instead of settling.

This is distinct from resonance with a periodic forcing input (a common but inaccurate popular explanation). The wind that morning was not gusting at a matching frequency — flutter is a feedback instability between the structure's motion and the airflow it generates, not a coincidence of two independent frequencies lining up.

## What the failure mode revealed

The bridge had shown milder vertical undulation since opening — enough that it earned the nickname "Galloping Gertie" from local drivers — but that motion was vertical bending, not the torsional mode that ultimately destroyed it. The design process of the period evaluated suspension bridges primarily for static and vertical loading and did not include a rigorous treatment of aerodynamic behavior; wind tunnel testing of bridge deck sections was not yet standard practice.

The aftermath directly changed civil engineering practice: post-collapse investigations, including work led by Theodore von Kármán, established aeroelastic flutter as a design consideration for long-span bridges. Wind tunnel testing of deck cross-sections became standard for major suspension and cable-stayed bridges going forward, and stiffer, deeper trusses or box girders became the norm for spans in this class.
